Lifestyle & Behavior Strategies

1. Dietary

a. Drink Plenty of Fluids! Normal fluid consumption is considered 64-70 oz of total fluids per day. A normal fluid consumption (not too little and not too much) helps maintain normal kidney and bladder function.

Benefits of a normal fluid consumption also include:
  • •  Decreasing Bladder Irritability leading to decreased bladder contractions
  • •  Prevention of Urinary Tract Infections
  • •  Prevention of Constipation

b. Limit or Decrease Bladder Irritants

  • Carbonated beverages
    or aspartame
  • Caffeine
  • Alcohol
  • Citrus Juices
  • Spicy foods

c. Increase high-fiber foods – this will help decrease constipation. Constipation plays a role in difficulty emptying your bladder and bowels and can contribute to urinary tract infections.

d. Maintain a healthy weight. Increased body weight has been associated with increased risk of both stress and urge UI.

2. Core Strengthening

Core stabilization exercises that are commonly part of a Pilates, Yoga or Tai Chi class enhance strength and stability to enhance pelvic floor function.

Core strengthening exercises that have been particularly studied to demonstrate similar pelvic floor muscle contraction squeeze pressures include bird dog, plank and leg lifts have been proven to generate pressures greater than those of concentrated pelvic floor muscle contractions.

3. Proper Toileting Techniques

When using the restroom, sit up straight and if possible, elevate your knees so that your knees are angled at a higher degree than your hips. You can utilize a step stool or even just elevate your heels to help elevate your knees. This allows for more complete bladder emptying. Remember, don’t push when using the restroom!

4. Double Voiding

Double voiding is simply waiting for a second bladder contraction after your initial urine stream. Normally, if we sit long enough, for example an extra 20-30 seconds, our bladder will contract again, allowing our bladder to empty more completely.

5. Urge Suppression Techniques: (Quick Flick Kegel Contractions)

Urge suppression techniques (or Quick Flicks) are used to help prevent urinary urgency, frequency and leakage associated with urgency. When you feel an urge, your first instinct is to rush to the bathroom. Instead, try and stand and perform 5 two-second pelvic floor muscle contractions (loop to Quick flick animation).

View Quick Flick Animation

Take a deep breath and continue standing still. Perform distraction techniques, such as counting backwards from 10. Then, slowly walk to the bathroom.

Urge Suppression Techniques are beneficial because as you contract your pelvic floor muscles, this then sends a reflex back to your bladder, allowing it to relax, decreasing unwanted bladder contractions, ultimately giving you more time to get to the bathroom in time without a leakage episode.

Goals of urge suppression techniques include:
  • 1. Being able to control of strong bladder urges.
  • 2. Decreasing how many times you need to use the restroom per day.
  • 3. Increases bladder capacity.

Myth

Incontinence is an inevitable part of being a woman or aging. It’s not! There are many strategies to reduce or eliminate urinary incontinence.

A strong pelvic floor improves quality of life: keeps organs in their anatomical place, contracts reflexively against intraabdominal pressure (laughing, coughing, sneezing, bending). The muscles in your pelvic floor have a tone than the other muscles in your body.

Types of Urinary Incontinence

Stress Urinary Incontinence (SUI)

Stress UI occurs when pelvic floor muscles no longer support the pelvic organs, most often the bladder. If pelvic floor muscles are weak, abrupt changes in intra-abdominal pressure are not equally transmitted to the bladder and urethra.

Symptoms of SUI

Symptoms of SUI: leakage occurring with laughing, coughing, sneezing, heavy lifting, exercise activities, bending, etc.

Urge Urinary Incontinence (UUI)

Urge UI is involuntary bladder contractions that create a sense of urinary urgency and frequency and is defined as urinary frequency more than 7x/day or getting up at night 2 or more times. Urge UI does not have to be associated with urinary leakage.

Symptoms of UUI

Urinary urgency associated with running water, seeing a toilet, cold weather, and when coming home from being out (Key in Lock Syndrome) and bathroom mapping (knowing the location of bathrooms in public places).

Mixed Urinary Incontinence (MUI)

This is a combination of both stress UI and Urge UI. Women experience both leakage associated with stress maneuvers as well as urinary urgency/frequency with or without urinary leakage.

Functional Urinary Incontinence (FUI)

Occurs when the bladder is working normally, however, there is an inability to get to the bathroom in time, whether due to physical, cognitive or environmental conditions.

Symptoms of FUI

Some of the causes of functional incontinence include problems with walking (arthritis or cerebral palsy) and problems with memory or learning (such as dementia and intellectual disability).

Behavioral Strategies

Pelvic Floor Muscle Exercises

Use it or lose it!

1. Comprised of Skeletal muscle – responds to exercise like any other muscle.

2. Endurance contractions incorporate the Type I muscle fibers (slow-twitch) and are referred to as “marathon runners”. The levator ani (puborectalis, iliococcygeus and pubococcygeus) comprise mostly slow-twitch muscle fibers and are responsible for sustained pelvic floor contractions. They comprise approximately 65% of the pelvic floor and are characterized by low force/low power/low speed.

3. Contraction of the pelvic floor muscles compresses around the urethra, increasing resistance to prevent leakage.

4. Stengthening these muscles increases muscle mass, allowing for a more effective contraction, whether to prevent stress or urge UI.

5. Provides proprioception to enhance sexuality.

Urge Suppression Techniques

1. When you feel the urge, don’t rush. Instead stand still and perform 5 quick pelvic floor contractions (referred to as Quick Flicks). Contraction of the pelvic floor causes a reflex back to the bladder and calms the bladder. Perform distraction techniques while standing or sitting (can count backwards from 10) and then slowly walk to the bathroom as the urge subsides.

2. Quick Flicks incorporate mostly the Type II muscle fibers (fast-twitch) referred to as “sprinters”. The ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus comprise mostly fast-twitch muscle fibers and are responsible for quick, rapid, short contractions.
